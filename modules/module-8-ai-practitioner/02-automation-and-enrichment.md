# 8.02 — Automation and Enrichment: What You Can Do

## Key Insight

AI tools do not eliminate the professional skill and judgment that office work requires. What they do is handle the mechanical and structural work faster, so that your time is spent on content, accuracy, and judgment — the parts that actually require you. Understanding what AI tools are genuinely useful for, and what they are not, is the difference between a productivity gain and a confidence problem.

---

## Microsoft Word

### First-Draft Generation from Bullet Notes

One of the most direct productivity gains is using AI to turn structured notes into a coherent first draft. Instead of staring at a blank page, you write bullet points covering the key points the document needs to make, then prompt the AI to draft from them.

This works because the structural challenge of "where do I start" and "how do I connect these points" is something the model handles efficiently. The content — the facts, the judgments, the professional positions — comes from you. The model provides the vehicle.

**An effective approach:** Write your notes in logical order, prompt the model with the audience and purpose, and ask for a first draft. Then edit the draft, replacing generalised language with your specific facts and removing any content the model added that you cannot verify.

### Restructuring Documents

AI tools can help with the structural problem of an existing document that has grown organically and no longer flows well. You can describe the current structure and the desired structure, paste a section, and ask the model to reorganise it.

This is useful for documents that have been written collaboratively over time and have an uneven register or a disconnected narrative. The model can impose consistency of structure without changing the substance — provided you review the output carefully.

### Summarising Long Documents

Summarisation is one of the most reliable AI use cases. Paste a long document (or use Copilot's direct integration with Word files) and ask for a summary tailored to a specific audience and purpose. The output is not always perfect — the model may overweight some points and underweight others — but it gives you a working draft that is usually faster to correct than to write from scratch.

Be specific about what kind of summary you need: key findings, action items, risks, a one-page overview, or a table of decisions. "Summarise this" produces a generic result; "summarise this for a non-technical committee, highlighting the three main recommendations and any items requiring a decision" produces something more directly useful.

### Style and Tone Adjustment

If you have a draft that is technically sound but written in an inappropriate register — too formal, too informal, too dense, too vague — AI tools can adjust the style while preserving the content. This is useful when adapting internal documents for external audiences, or when consolidating contributions from multiple authors into a consistent voice.

Always review tone adjustments carefully: the model may inadvertently soften a position you intended to state firmly, or sharpen a statement you intended to leave open.

### Proofreading Beyond Spellcheck

Standard spellcheck catches spelling errors and some grammar issues. AI tools can provide a higher-order review: checking for consistency (does the document use the same terminology throughout?), identifying sentences that are grammatically correct but hard to read, flagging where the argument loses coherence, and suggesting where a paragraph should be broken up.

This is a supporting function, not a replacement for careful reading. The model can miss contextual errors — a sentence that is grammatically correct but factually wrong will not be flagged.

## Microsoft Excel

### Formula Suggestion and Explanation

Many Excel users know what calculation they want to perform but are uncertain which formula to use or how to construct it. AI tools can bridge this gap: describe in plain English what you want to calculate, and the model can suggest the formula and explain what each part does.

This is particularly useful for functions that are powerful but unintuitive (XLOOKUP, INDEX/MATCH, array formulas, SUMPRODUCT). You describe the business logic; the model translates it into syntax.

Always test the suggested formula against a small dataset before relying on it for important work. Formula suggestions are usually correct in structure but may need adjustment for your specific data layout.

### Data Cleaning

Inconsistent data is one of the most common obstacles in Excel work. AI tools can suggest approaches to standardising date formats, correcting capitalisation, splitting combined fields, or identifying duplicates. In some cases, Copilot in Excel can make these changes directly; in other cases, the model describes the approach and you implement it.

For large datasets where manual cleaning is impractical, this can be a significant time saver. Verify a sample of the cleaned data manually before using it in analysis.

### Generating Chart Summaries in Plain English

A chart or table conveys information to people who can read it analytically, but not everyone who receives a spreadsheet has that comfort with data. AI tools can generate plain-English summaries of charts and tables that describe the trend, the key figures, and what the data shows. This is useful for dashboards that need an accompanying narrative, or for reports where the data is for a mixed audience.

Verify that the model's interpretation of the data matches your own reading before using the summary. AI tools can misread trends, particularly if the data has unusual patterns or the chart formatting is ambiguous.

### Identifying Patterns in Data

AI tools can help you notice patterns in data that are not immediately obvious: comparing rows across categories, identifying outliers, flagging month-on-month changes, or describing the distribution of values in a column. This is not statistical analysis — it is a structured observation that supplements your own reading of the data.

For decisions that depend on the analysis being correct, verify AI-identified patterns against the underlying data before relying on them.

### Building PivotTable Explanations

PivotTables are powerful but can be difficult to explain to someone who did not build them. AI tools can help you draft a plain-English explanation of what a PivotTable shows, what its row and column groupings represent, and how to interpret the values. This supports the handover of spreadsheet work and the documentation of analytical methodology.

## Microsoft PowerPoint

### Generating Slide Outlines from Documents

Given a document or a set of notes, AI tools can suggest a slide structure — how many slides, what each slide should cover, and what the narrative arc should be. This is the structural planning work that many people find time-consuming before opening PowerPoint.

The output is a starting point for discussion, not a finished presentation. The model will produce a structurally sound outline, but it does not know your audience, your speaking style, or the specific emphasis your message requires.

### Speaker Notes from Slide Content

Once you have a set of slides, AI tools can draft speaker notes for each slide — expanding the bullet points on the slide into the full points you might make when presenting. This is useful for presentations that will be delivered by someone other than the person who built the slides, or for building a script as a starting point that you then adapt.

Review speaker notes carefully for accuracy: the model is inferring what you might say from the slide content, and it may fill gaps with plausible-sounding but incorrect statements.

### Visual Layout Suggestions

AI tools integrated into PowerPoint (Copilot and the Designer feature) can suggest visual layouts for individual slides — recommending how to arrange text and images, offering alternative designs, and applying consistent formatting. This is a faster path to a visually coherent presentation than manually adjusting each slide.

These are suggestions, not prescriptions. Choose layouts that match your organisation's branding and the formality of the occasion.

## Outlook and Teams

### Email Drafting from Notes

Writing effective emails takes time, particularly for messages that need to balance directness with diplomacy. AI tools can turn brief notes into a draft email, saving the initial writing time. You then adjust the draft for tone, accuracy, and any nuance that requires your judgment.

This works best for routine but important emails — meeting requests, follow-ups, internal announcements. It is less suitable for sensitive communications where the precise wording is critical and error has significant consequences; in those cases, the AI draft is a useful starting point but every sentence needs active consideration.

### Meeting Summary Generation

Teams Copilot can summarise a recorded meeting — who spoke, what was discussed, what decisions were made. This addresses a genuine pain point: long meetings produce no persistent record unless someone takes comprehensive notes.

AI-generated meeting summaries are useful but not infallible. The model may misattribute a comment to the wrong speaker, miss decisions made in crosstalk, or underweight a point that was important but briefly stated. For decisions with significant consequences, verify the summary against any notes taken during the meeting.

### Action Item Extraction

Extracting action items from a meeting or a long email thread is one of the most reliable AI use cases in the communication context. The model is looking for statements of commitment or assignment — "I'll send the report by Friday", "Can you follow up with the client?" — and compiling them into a list. This is structural pattern recognition that the model handles well.

Confirm the extracted action items with the relevant parties before treating them as the definitive record.

## Practice Exercise: The ICIO Prompt for Your Own Work

Take one real task from your current workload that involves creating or editing a document, analysing data, or drafting a communication. It does not have to be a large task — something that typically takes you 30–60 minutes is ideal.

Construct a prompt for this task using the four-pillar ICIO structure:

**Instruction (I):** Write one sentence stating precisely what you want the AI to do. Use specific verbs: draft, summarise, rewrite, extract, compare. Avoid "help me with" or "do something about."

**Context (C):** Write 2–4 sentences providing background: who will read the output, what register is appropriate (formal, informal, technical, plain English), what constraints apply (length, format, jurisdiction, audience).

**Input Data (I):** Identify the content you will give to the model. If it contains client names, personal information, or confidential data, refer to Section 03 before proceeding — you may need to de-identify it first.

**Output Indicator (O):** Specify exactly what you want back: a paragraph, a table, a bulleted list, a numbered schedule. Specify length, tense, or any explicit exclusions.

Test the prompt. Compare the output to what you would have produced manually in the same time. Identify one specific improvement you would make to the prompt to get closer to the output you need.

## What AI Tools Are Not Good At

Being clear about limitations is as important as understanding capabilities.

**AI tools do not know your specific facts unless you tell them.** The model does not have access to your client file, your project data, or your organisation's specific situation. If the task requires accurate reference to specific facts, those facts must appear in your prompt.

**AI tools can be confidently wrong.** A model can generate a plausible-sounding statement about a regulatory requirement, a calculation, or a historical fact that is simply incorrect. The text will look and read like an accurate statement. This is known as hallucination. Never rely on AI-generated facts without verifying them against an authoritative source.

**AI tools do not replace professional judgment.** A model can help you draft a client advice letter. It cannot apply professional judgment to whether the advice is appropriate, whether there is a conflict, or whether the matter has implications the draft has missed. The professional responsibility remains yours.
