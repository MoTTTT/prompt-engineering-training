# 8.04 — Governance and Compliance

## Key Insight

Governance is not what your organisation does to slow you down — it is what protects you, your clients, and your organisation when something goes wrong. The office professional who understands the rules is not just safer; they are better placed to use AI tools with confidence, because they know which uses are clearly acceptable and which require care. Uncertainty is not a reason to avoid AI tools — it is a reason to ask.

---

## What an AI Use Policy Covers

Most organisations that have deployed AI tools formally have an AI use policy — a document that sets out the rules. If your organisation has one, read it. The sections that are most relevant to office professionals:

### Approved Tools

The policy will typically list the AI tools that have been assessed, approved, and licensed for use. Using a tool not on this list for work purposes — even a well-known tool that you use personally — may not be permitted without prior approval. The reason is not bureaucratic caution: unapproved tools have not been assessed for data protection compliance, may not have a Data Processing Agreement in place, and may have terms that are incompatible with your obligations to clients.

If you want to use a tool that is not on the approved list, raise a request with your IT or data protection function. Do not assume approval from silence.

### Prohibited Uses

The policy will typically specify categories of use that are not permitted. Common prohibitions include: entering client personal data into unapproved tools; using AI tools to draft communications that will be sent without human review; using AI tools for decisions that affect individuals (hiring, performance, benefits); and using AI tools to generate content that will be presented as having been written by a specific named person without disclosure.

### Data Classification Rules

Organisations that classify information — confidential, internal, public, restricted — will typically specify which classification levels can be processed by which AI tools. Content classified as confidential or restricted typically cannot be used with cloud AI tools without specific approval.

### Incident Reporting

The policy will specify what counts as an AI-related incident and how to report it. An incident might include: accidentally sending client personal data to an unapproved cloud tool; receiving AI-generated output that contains what appears to be another user's confidential data; or discovering that a colleague is using an AI tool in a way that violates the policy.

## GDPR and POPIA: What They Mean for You at Your Desk

Data protection law applies to how you handle personal information in your work, not just to the systems your IT team builds. As an office professional, you are a person who processes personal data on behalf of your organisation, and the law's obligations apply to you directly.

### What Counts as Personal Data

Personal data is any information that relates to an identified or identifiable living individual. In practice, for office professionals, this means: names, email addresses, phone numbers, home addresses, ID numbers, dates of birth, salary and financial details, health information, and any other detail that could be used to identify a specific person — including in combination with other details.

Under both GDPR (which applies across the EU, and to any organisation processing data about EU individuals) and POPIA (which applies in South Africa), health data, financial data, and political and religious information are special categories that attract heightened protection. These are categories you will encounter regularly in professional services work.

### The Data Minimisation Principle

Both GDPR and POPIA require that you process only the personal data that is necessary for the specific purpose. In the context of AI tool use, this means: before using any AI tool with personal data, ask whether the task actually requires that data. If you can accomplish the task with less — or with de-identified data — that is what the law expects.

A practical example: if you are using an AI tool to improve the structure and language of a report, the tool does not need the client's name or ID number to do its job. Remove them. Use them only when the specific task requires them — and when the tool and your organisation's policy permit it.

### Why You Need a Lawful Basis

Data protection law requires that there is a lawful basis for processing personal data. Your organisation's lawyers and data protection officer have identified the lawful bases that apply to your firm's various activities. The relevant point for AI tool use is that your professional engagement with a client gives you a basis to process their data for the purpose of providing the service you have agreed to provide — it does not automatically give you a basis to route their data through a third-party AI provider who is not part of that agreement.

This is why approved tools with Data Processing Agreements matter. The DPA establishes the contractual framework that brings the AI provider inside the lawful processing chain. Without it, the processing is likely unlawful regardless of how beneficial the output might be.

### Rights That Individuals Hold

Data subjects — the people whose data you process — have rights under both GDPR and POPIA. These include the right to know their data is being processed, the right to access the data held about them, and the right to have incorrect data corrected. If your firm uses AI tools to process client data, clients may ask questions about this. Your firm's privacy notice and data protection officer can help you respond correctly.

## The EU AI Act in Plain Language

The EU AI Act is the first comprehensive law specifically governing AI systems. It applies to AI used in the EU, regardless of where the provider is based. Its direct impact on office professionals is limited but worth understanding.

### The Risk Tiers — Simplified

The Act classifies AI uses into categories based on risk:

**Prohibited uses:** Certain uses of AI are simply banned — systems that manipulate people subliminally, AI-based social scoring by governments, and (with limited exceptions) real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces. You are unlikely to encounter these in office use.

**High-risk uses:** AI systems that make or significantly influence decisions that affect individuals in areas such as employment, education, access to essential services, or benefits determinations. If your organisation uses AI to help make decisions about hiring, performance assessment, or client eligibility for services, this tier applies and requires specific oversight and documentation. As an office professional, the most important thing is to know whether the AI tool you are using is in this category — and if it is, to ensure that a human reviews every decision before it is acted on.

**Limited-risk uses:** Customer-facing chatbots and AI-generated content fall here. The main obligation is disclosure: users must be told they are interacting with AI or receiving AI-generated content. If you are using an AI tool to draft communications that will be sent to clients or the public, your organisation may have obligations about how to disclose this.

**Minimal-risk uses:** Most AI tools used for internal productivity — writing assistance, summarisation, formula suggestion — fall into this category. The Act places no mandatory requirements here, though your organisation's own policy may.

### What This Means for You

The EU AI Act is primarily a framework for organisations building or deploying AI systems — not for office professionals using approved tools. Its practical impact on your day-to-day work is that:

- Your organisation may be required to implement human oversight for certain AI-assisted decisions
- Your organisation may have disclosure obligations about AI-generated content sent to clients
- If your organisation operates in the EU or serves EU individuals, the compliance considerations in this module are not optional

If you are asked to use an AI tool to make or contribute to a decision that affects a specific individual — whether they receive a service, are hired, or are assessed in some way — ask whether there is a human review step. There should be.

## How to Raise a Concern

Knowing when and how to raise a concern is part of responsible AI use. The following situations warrant escalation, not silence.

### If You Think an AI Tool Is Being Misused

Examples: a colleague is using an unapproved consumer AI tool with client data; an AI tool is being used to make decisions that affect individuals without human review; AI-generated content is being presented as original work without disclosure.

What to do: raise it with your manager or your firm's data protection officer. You do not need to be certain that something is wrong — a genuine concern is sufficient reason to ask. Most organisations have reporting channels specifically for data protection concerns.

### If You Have Accidentally Sent Confidential Data to a Cloud Service

This happens. The right response is prompt disclosure, not concealment.

Immediately stop using the tool for that content. Note what was sent — which content, which tool, approximately when. Report it to your manager and data protection officer as quickly as possible. Under GDPR and POPIA, data incidents may need to be reported to the relevant supervisory authority within 72 hours if there is a risk to individuals. The 72-hour clock starts from when the organisation becomes aware. Early reporting gives your firm time to assess the risk and respond appropriately.

Do not attempt to assess the severity yourself and decide not to report it. That assessment belongs to your data protection officer, not to you.

### If an AI Tool Produces Output That Appears to Contain Someone Else's Data

There have been documented cases of AI tools, under certain conditions, regenerating content from previous users' conversations. If you receive AI output that appears to contain personal information about an individual you do not recognise — names, emails, details of someone else's work — stop, do not use the output, and report it to your data protection officer.

## The Business Case for Responsible Use

Responsible AI use is not just a compliance obligation — it is a competitive and professional asset.

**Client trust.** Professional services firms hold client data under a duty of confidentiality that is foundational to the relationship. A data incident caused by careless AI tool use can end client relationships and expose the firm to professional indemnity claims. The reputational damage from a disclosed data breach typically exceeds the financial cost.

**Professional indemnity.** Most professional indemnity policies were written before AI tools became routine. Some insurers are now asking questions about AI use practices. Using AI tools in ways that violate data protection law or professional conduct rules may affect your firm's coverage.

**Regulatory exposure.** Data protection regulators have issued significant fines for GDPR violations. POPIA enforcement by the South African Information Regulator is active. A regulator investigating a complaint about AI-assisted data processing will look at whether the organisation had a policy, whether staff were trained, and whether the policy was followed. Having been through training such as this module is part of that record.

**Your personal position.** Individuals can face personal liability for data protection breaches in some circumstances. Acting in good faith, following your organisation's policy, and escalating concerns promptly is the best protection available to you as an individual.

The message is straightforward: using AI tools responsibly — within approved tools, with appropriate data handling, following the rules in this module — is not a constraint on productivity. It is what allows the productivity gains of AI to be realised sustainably, without the exposure that careless use creates.
